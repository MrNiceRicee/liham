# Palamig: Filipino Street Drinks

> Before pearl coolers and milk tea, there was *samalamig*.

Walk any Filipino street during summer and you'll see them — massive glass jars lined up on a cart, each one a different color, sweating in the heat. Sago't gulaman, buko pandan, calamansi juice, fruit salad. These are **palamig** — cold drinks sold by street vendors to survive the 30-40°C dry season from March to May.

The word *samalamig* comes from *sa malamig* — "for the cold." These drinks are older than any franchise, passed down through generations of vendors and home cooks. They're shared at family gatherings, offered to guests as a welcome, bought between classes, sipped on commutes. Palamig is not a beverage category. It's Filipino hospitality in a glass.

## Sago't Gulaman

The most iconic palamig. Tapioca pearls, grass jelly cubes, and brown sugar syrup over cold water.

*Adapted from [Panlasang Pinoy](https://panlasangpinoy.com/sago-at-gulaman/) by Vanjo Merano.*

### Ingredients

| Component | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tapioca pearls (sago) | 1 cup | Cook until translucent |
| Red gelatin powder | 1/2 oz | Unflavored |
| Water (for boiling) | 6 cups | 4 for sago, 2 for gulaman |
| Brown sugar | 1 cup | For the arnibal syrup |
| Water (for syrup) | 1 cup | |
| Vanilla extract | 1 tsp | |
| White sugar | 2 tbsp | For the gelatin |

### Steps

1. **Cook the sago:** boil 4 cups water, add tapioca pearls, cook 8 minutes on medium heat. Reduce to low until translucent and chewy
2. **Make the gulaman:** boil 2 cups water, add sugar and gelatin, stir until dissolved. Cool, refrigerate until set, cube
3. **Brew the arnibal:** combine brown sugar, 1 cup water, and vanilla. Boil 1 minute, stirring
4. **Assemble:** sago + cubed gulaman + 3 tbsp arnibal + 1 cup cold water

> **Tip:** Rinse cooked sago in cold water immediately — stops them from sticking.

## Calamansi Juice

The Filipino lemonade. Smaller, rounder, and more fragrant than any lemon.

The golden ratio: $3 : 1 : 0.5$ (water : calamansi : simple syrup).

For a pitcher: $V_s = V_w / 6$

- 12 calamansi, halved and squeezed
- 4 cups cold water
- Simple syrup to taste (start with $2/3$ cup)
- Ice — the more the better

> Roll each calamansi on the counter before cutting. More juice.

## Tsokolate

Traditional hot chocolate — thick, dark, frothed with a wooden *batidor*.

### Tablea (Cacao Tablets)

Pure cacao, roasted and ground into tablets. No sugar — that's your call. Dissolve $1$ tablet in $200$ml hot water or milk.

```
frothing technique:
  1. hold batidor handle between palms
  2. roll back and forth rapidly
  3. lift slightly as froth builds
  4. stop when foam holds its shape
```

> Pair with **pan de sal** fresh from the corner bakery, torn open and dunked.

## Lambanog

Coconut wine distilled from coconut sap. Crystal clear, dangerously smooth.

**ABV:** $40\%$–$45\%$, homebrew can reach $60\%$

- Bubblegum (pink, sweet, popular at fiestas)
- Raisin-infused (darker, richer)
- Plain (the purist's choice)

> **Warning:** Lambanog hits different. The coconut smoothness is deceptive.
