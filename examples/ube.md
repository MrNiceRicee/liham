# Ube: Not a Trend

> *"There's a soul to its consumption. It's part of the culture, it's part of our heritage."*
> — Jeremy Villanueva, executive chef

Ube went viral in the 2010s. Suddenly it was everywhere — doughnuts in New York, lattes in Los Angeles, ice cream at Trader Joe's. The internet discovered purple and couldn't stop posting about it.

But ube is not a color. It's not a trend. It is a piece of Filipino heritage that predates colonization.

## 400 Years Before the Hype

Ube first appeared in the **1613 Tagalog-Spanish dictionary** as *uvi*. In Bohol, the original variety — *kinampay* — is considered sacred, having helped communities survive wartime and drought. Root crops like ube, kamote, and cassava are the foundation of the Filipino diet, providing sustained energy across generations.

Before American colonization in 1898, halaya was made with fresh **carabao milk**. Condensed and evaporated milk came later — a colonial substitution that stuck.

When the Manila Social Club's ube doughnut went viral in 2015, it triggered a conversation Filipino-Americans had been having for years: a crop with centuries of cultural significance was being reduced to its aesthetic. The purple is not the point.

## Ube Halaya (Purple Yam Jam)

The most common preparation — boiled, mashed, stirred with sweetened milk and butter into a thick paste. The base layer for everything.

*Adapted from [Panlasang Pinoy](https://panlasangpinoy.com/simple-ube-halaya-recipe/) by Vanjo Merano.*

### Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Purple yam (ube) | 1 lb | Grated |
| Coconut milk | 2 cups | Full fat |
| Granulated sugar | 3/4 cup | |
| Cooking oil | 1 tsp | For greasing the mold |

> **Traditional note:** before 1898, halaya was made with fresh carabao milk — condensed and evaporated milk came later.

### Prep Checklist

- [ ] Grate the purple yam
- [ ] Grease the llanera with cooking oil
- [ ] Prepare a heavy-bottomed pot
- [x] Clear your schedule — this takes **patience**

### Steps

1. Pour coconut milk in a pot and bring to a boil
2. Add sugar and stir until completely dissolved
3. Add the grated purple yam
4. Cook on **low to medium heat**, stirring continuously — 20 to 30 minutes
5. Keep stirring until the texture gets thick and pulls away from the sides
6. Transfer to a greased llanera, flatten the top
7. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate

---

> **Lola's tip:** The stirring never stops. If your arm is tired, switch hands. If both arms are tired, you're almost done.

## Where Ube Lives

Ube is not one dessert. It is a **platform** — the ingredient that ties Filipino sweets together.

| Dessert | Role |
|---|---|
| Halo-halo | Base layer |
| Ensaymada | Filling and topping |
| Pandesal | Spread |
| Ice cream | The star |
| Leche flan | Swirled in |
| Halaya with cheese | The salty-sweet classic |

## Storage

| Method | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigerated | 5-7 days | Covered with plastic wrap |
| Frozen | 3 months | Thaw overnight in fridge |
| Room temp | Same day | Tropical heat is unforgiving |
